% ABSTRACT

\begin{abstract}
\noindent\textbf{Motivation:}
Gene-regulatory-network (GRN) inference from single-cell transcriptomics is
increasingly used to prioritize regulatory hypotheses, yet most benchmarks
evaluate accuracy under fixed preprocessing without testing whether rankings
survive realistic analytical perturbations, and most published fragility claims
are made under a single scorer family.

\noindent\textbf{Results:}
We audit GRN edge rankings across five fragility axes---cell count,
clustering resolution, rare-cell reliability, donor generalization, and
integration sensitivity---on four Tabula Sapiens tissues, one
non-Tabula-Sapiens external lung cohort (Travaglini et al.\ 2020
SmartSeq2), and one disease cohort (Delorey et al.\ 2021 COVID-19
lung atlas, 116{,}313 cells, 27 donors), using five scorer families:
Pearson correlation, mutual information, GRNBoost2, GENIE3, and
scGPT attention.
Our central findings are that:
(i)~cluster-resolution stability under a bounded-drift
Resolution-Sensitivity-Score sits 9--13 standard deviations below a
rank-permutation null, yet every cohort fails a within-coarse
constrained shuffle, so dual-null calibration recommends coarse or
hybrid clustering across the full 66-point weight simplex;
(ii)~the rare-vs-abundant reliability-gate gap direction is preserved
at 100\% of 5{,}880 reasonable threshold combinations, establishing
that the 0\% rare passage rate is not an artifact of threshold choice;
(iii)~the minimum valid cell count (MVCC) is strongly scorer-dependent:
under scGPT attention it exceeds 3{,}000 cells, whereas under
Pearson-over-highly-variable-genes it is 200 cells for four of six
cohorts and 500 cells for the two largest ones (primary immune and
Delorey COVID lung), and it is anchor-invariant;
(iv)~6--8 donors are needed for immune generalization under strict
cells-per-donor balancing, and fewer under looser balancing;
(v)~integration method choice is not neutral: Harmony preserves
rankings reasonably (Spearman 0.63--0.87, sign-flip 6--17\%), scVI
moderately perturbs them (Spearman 0.36--0.57, sign-flip 18--30\%),
and Scanorama on kidney and lung produces near-random rankings
(Spearman $\approx 0$, sign-flip 44--47\%);
(vi)~scGPT attention rankings are statistically uncorrelated with
classical-scorer rankings on matched kidney cells (Spearman
$\rho \leq 0.20$, top-100 Jaccard $\leq 0.06$), so within-scorer
fragility profiles do not transfer across scorer families.
A degree-preserving edge-rewire null is cleared at $p = 0.02$ on every
cohort, rebutting the possibility that the within-coarse-shuffle
failures reflect null over-stringency rather than genuine signal loss.
No single cohort passes all five axes under calibrated criteria.

archived at \url{https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.20009065}.

\end{abstract}

% 1. INTRODUCTION

\section{Introduction}

Single-cell RNA-sequencing has enabled transcriptome-wide inference of gene
regulatory networks (GRNs). Classical correlation- and tree-based scorers
such as SCENIC~\citep{Aibar2017}, GRNBoost2~\citep{Moerman2019},
GENIE3~\citep{Huynh-Thu2010}, and mutual-information /
ARACNe-family methods define one end of the methodological spectrum.
At the other end, attention-derived edge scores from single-cell foundation
models such as scGPT~\citep{Cui2024} offer a scalable, representation-rich
alternative. All of these methods rank transcription-factor--target
(TF--target) edges and interpret top-ranked pairs as regulatory hypotheses,
but confidence in those rankings depends on analytical choices that are
rarely stress-tested in practice.

Most published GRN benchmarks~\citep{Pratapa2020, Chen2018} evaluate accuracy
against a single reference database under fixed preprocessing. A complementary
and largely unasked question is whether the same edges would be prioritized
if the analyst used a different cell count, clustering resolution, integration
method, or donor cohort. The practical consequence is that two groups
analyzing the same atlas can reach different biological conclusions simply
by making different preprocessing decisions, without either being
demonstrably wrong. Recent atlas-level integration benchmarks
\citep{Luecken2022, Tran2020} have documented that batch-correction methods
perturb downstream analyses, but the specific impact on TF--target ranking
stability has not been systematically characterised.

We address this gap by conducting a systematic multi-axis robustness audit
of TF--target edge rankings across five scorer families (Pearson correlation,
mutual information, GRNBoost2, GENIE3, and scGPT attention) and six cohorts
(four Tabula Sapiens tissues, one external-lab lung dataset, and one
COVID-19 disease atlas). Our audit spans
five axes that correspond to the most common sources of analytical variation
in single-cell GRN workflows:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Cell-count subsampling:} How many cells are needed for stable
      rankings, is that threshold dependent on the reference-anchor cell
      count, and do prior-constrained policies rescue small-sample regimes?
\item \textbf{Cluster-resolution sensitivity:} Does switching between coarse
      and fine clustering change which edges are prioritized, and does the
      conclusion survive rank-permutation nulls, weight-choice sensitivity,
      gene-dimension sweeps, and a triple-null calibration?
\item \textbf{Rare-cell-type reliability:} Do rare cell types yield
      mechanistic edges that pass the same confidence gates as abundant
      types, and how robust is that gap to the choice of gate thresholds?
\item \textbf{Donor generalization:} How many donors are needed for rankings
      to transfer to held-out individuals, and is the threshold dependent
      on cells-per-donor balancing choices?
\item \textbf{Integration-method sensitivity:} Do batch-correction
      methods (Harmony, scVI, and Scanorama) alter edge rankings, and do
      any of those changes improve biological validity as measured by
      perturbation-backed evidence rather than literature precision alone?
\end{enumerate}

Beyond the per-axis questions, we ask an orthogonal one: \emph{do
fragility conclusions drawn under one scorer transfer to another?}
We find that scGPT attention rankings are statistically uncorrelated
with Pearson / mutual information / GRNBoost2 / GENIE3 rankings on the
same cells and the same edge universe ($\rho \leq 0.20$, top-100
Jaccard $\leq 0.06$), which generalises the message from ``rankings
are fragile under preprocessing perturbations'' to ``rankings are
fragile under preprocessing perturbations \emph{and} scorer choice.''

Each axis was developed through an adversarial-review loop in which an
AI-assisted review agent systematically challenged the initial analysis for
methodological weaknesses (confounded null designs, overfitted
hyperparameters, hidden data leakage, uninterpretable composite metrics,
panel-selection arbitrariness), corrective experiments were designed and
executed, and claims were updated accordingly. A full protocol description
(base model, prompt templates, round-by-round logs, and a
pre-registered audit of 27 agent-raised issues under a frozen rubric)
is provided in Supplementary Methods~S2.

The remainder of this paper describes the shared experimental setup
(Section~2), presents per-axis results (Section~3), reports a
scorer-divergence analysis (Section~3.6), analyses cross-axis
interactions (Section~3.7), and provides a practical guidelines table
for practitioners (Section~4).

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Datasets, preprocessing, and panels}

All analyses used processed single-cell datasets from the Tabula
Sapiens atlas~\citep{TabulaSapiens2022}, one external lung cohort
(Travaglini et al.\ 2020 SmartSeq2), and one COVID-19 disease lung
atlas (Delorey et al.\ 2021~\citep{Delorey2021}; Table~\ref{tab:datasets}).
Datasets were preprocessed using Scanpy~\citep{Wolf2018} following established
best practices~\citep{Luecken2019}. The primary normalization was
depth-normalization (``\texttt{normalize\_total}'' with ``\texttt{target\_sum}='' $10^4$)
followed by \texttt{log1p}, with the top 600 highly-variable genes selected
using the Seurat v3 flavor. Two alternative normalization schemes---median-ratio
size-factor normalization and Pearson residuals~\citep{Lause2021}---were
evaluated as an ablation (Supplementary Fig.~S6) and produced the same
calibrated recommendation on every axis we examined. Every pipeline run
stamps the full preprocessing parameter set into its provenance record;
numerical reproducibility is therefore traceable to an explicit
configuration file rather than to narrative prose.

TF--target edge panels used in axes with panel-restricted scoring were
loaded from a shared registry (Supplementary Table~S1) that resolves each
panel to an exact edge list at runtime. Named panels include:
``\emph{primary}'' (DoRothEA~\citep{GarciaAlonso2019} confidence A/B
$\cap$ TRRUST~\citep{Han2018}, 3{,}384 edges before gene-universe
restriction); ``\emph{dorothea\_ab}''; ``\emph{trrust}'';
``\emph{hematopoiesis\_76$\times$108}'' (76 hematopoiesis TFs $\times$ 108
immune / cross-tissue targets = 8{,}208 directed edges, used in
Axis~4); and ``\emph{shared\_36}'' (the TRRUST-intersected
activation/repression panel used for Axis~3 cross-dataset sensitivity).
Axis~2 operates on all HVG pairs rather than a curated panel so that the
coarse-versus-fine question is closed under a shared edge universe.

\subsection{Edge scorers}

Every axis pipeline accepts a pluggable ``edge scorer'' via a shared
interface, so the same preprocessing and null library feeds every
methodological choice. Five scorers are implemented:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Pearson correlation} on column-standardized expression.
      Sign of correlation is tracked separately from the magnitude score.
\item \textbf{Mutual information} using scikit-learn's
      ``\texttt{mutual\_info\_regression}'' (Kraskov $k$-nearest-neighbour
      estimator, $k=3$, seed fixed).
\item \textbf{GRNBoost2}~\citep{Moerman2019}, implemented as per-target
      gradient-boosted regression importance (500 estimators, depth 3,
      learning rate 0.01, sqrt feature subsampling). Single-threaded
      scikit-learn backend to avoid the dask/multiprocessing fork issues
      in our environment.
\item \textbf{GENIE3}~\citep{Huynh-Thu2010}, implemented as per-target
      random-forest regression importance (500 estimators, sqrt feature
      subsampling).
\item \textbf{scGPT attention}~\citep{Cui2024}, extracted from the public
      \emph{whole-human} checkpoint. For each cell we tokenise the panel
      genes, forward-pass through the twelve transformer layers, and
      average attention weights across all layers and heads; edge scores
      are then the symmetrised cell-wise mean of attention between each
      TF and target token. The extractor bypasses scGPT's torchtext
      dependency (which is incompatible with our torch version) via a
      lightweight vocabulary stub and remaps the FlashAttention-trained
      ``\texttt{Wqkv}'' checkpoint keys to the standard
      ``\texttt{in\_proj}'' format used by
      ``\texttt{nn.MultiheadAttention}''.
\end{itemize}

When a figure or statistic is produced by a single scorer, the scorer is
stated explicitly in the caption. Section~3.6 reports the pairwise
agreement matrix between all five scorers.

\subsection{Metric definitions}

To make every claim traceable, we define the shared metrics here once.
Let $\mathbf{r}_a, \mathbf{r}_b \in \mathbb{Z}^N$ be the edge ranks from two
scoring runs on a shared edge universe of size $N$, and let
$T_a^k, T_b^k$ be their top-$k$ index sets.

\begin{align*}
\text{overlap}_k      &= \tfrac{|T_a^k \cap T_b^k|}{k}, \\
\text{Jaccard}_k      &= \tfrac{|T_a^k \cap T_b^k|}{|T_a^k \cup T_b^k|}, \\
\text{drift\_norm}_k  &= \min\!\left(1,\,\tfrac{\operatorname{median}_{i \in T_a^k \cap T_b^k}|r_{a,i} - r_{b,i}|}{k}\right), \\
\text{RSS}_k          &= w_o\,(1 - \text{overlap}_k) + w_j\,(1 - \text{Jaccard}_k) + w_d \cdot \text{drift\_norm}_k.
\end{align*}

The bounded ``drift\_norm'' follows directly from the observation
that the worst-case rank change inside a top-$k$ set is exactly $k$,
so dividing by $k$ gives a statistic in $[0, 1]$, and the explicit
$\min(1,\cdot)$ clip guards against numerical edge cases. Unless noted,
$k = 1{,}000$ and weights $(w_o, w_j, w_d) = (0.4, 0.3, 0.3)$;
weights are exposed as arguments so that the simplex sensitivity
sweep reported in Section~3.2 can traverse all non-negative triples
summing to 1.

``\textbf{Spearman rank stability}'' is the Spearman $\rho$ between two
edge-score vectors. ``\textbf{Null-separation AUC}'' is the area under
the ROC separating observed edge scores from permutation-null scores.
``\textbf{Tail gap}'' is the difference between the top
5\% quantile of observed and null. ``\textbf{Sign-flip rate}'' is the
fraction of edges whose score-sign changes between two runs.
``\textbf{Mean absolute rank shift}'' is $\operatorname{mean}_i |r_{a,i} - r_{b,i}|$.
A null family is said to be ``\emph{cleared}'' when the empirical
$p$-value of the observed statistic against that family is
$\leq 0.05$ with $\geq 19$ permutations (the minimum count at which the
empirical $p$-value floor can reach 0.05).

\subsection{Axis~1: Cell-count subsampling}

Cell-count robustness was evaluated on all cohorts for which a
sufficient cell count was available (kidney, lung, immune,
immune-invariant, external lung, and Delorey COVID lung) at cell
counts $\{200, 500, 1{,}000, 2{,}000, 3{,}000, 5{,}000, 8{,}000,
15{,}000\}$ (clipped to dataset maximum) with eight independent
subsamples per size. An \emph{anchor ablation} was added to address
the concern that using a 3{,}000-cell reference is circular: each
dataset's MVCC was computed independently against a 3{,}000-cell and
an 8{,}000-cell anchor (plus a 15{,}000-cell anchor for Delorey,
whose cell count permits it). Per-cell, per-anchor top-$k$ Jaccard
and overlap were computed at $k \in \{100, 500, 1{,}000, 5{,}000\}$,
plus top-1\%, top-5\%, and top-10\% proportions of the edge universe,
and per-target top-5 and top-10 overlaps.

The Minimum Valid Cell Count (MVCC) is the smallest cell count at which
the mean top-1{,}000 Jaccard exceeds 0.5 against the reference anchor.
We report MVCC per (dataset, anchor, scorer) triple so that both
anchor-dependence and scorer-dependence can be read directly from
the results. Benjamini--Hochberg correction was
applied to the family of per-(policy, cell-count) $p$-values testing
whether prior-constrained rankings differ from matched random-gene-set
controls.

\subsection{Axis~2: Cluster-resolution sensitivity}

Coarse-versus-fine clustering sensitivity was assessed across all
six cohorts in Table~\ref{tab:datasets}. Coarse labels corresponded to broad cell classes or tissue
compartments; fine labels used precomputed Leiden subclusters enforced
as strict hierarchical refinements of the coarse partition. Edge scores
combined global and per-group maximum absolute Pearson correlation,
$S = 0.6\,R_{\text{global}} + 0.4\,R_{\text{group\,max}}$, over the top 600
high-variance genes. Agreement between coarse-derived and fine-derived
rankings was quantified by the bounded-drift RSS defined in Section~2.2.

Three null families were applied in a tiered calibration:
(i) a \emph{global cell-label shuffle}; (ii) a \emph{within-coarse
constrained shuffle}; and (iii) a \emph{degree-preserving bipartite
rewire} on the TF--target score matrix (49 permutations per family).
A base recommendation derived from the RSS threshold---``fine'' if
$\text{RSS} \leq 0.30$, ``hybrid'' if $\leq 0.45$, otherwise
``coarse''---was downgraded one level per failed null family
(fine $\to$ hybrid $\to$ coarse). The degree-preserving null serves as
a permissive bracket that isolates whether the within-coarse failure
reflects null over-stringency or genuine over-partitioning.

In addition, the RSS weights were swept over the full 66-point simplex
at step 0.1, an empirical null distribution was constructed from 1{,}000
rank permutations per cohort, and the HVG count was swept over
$\{300, 600, 900, 1{,}200\}$. All three sensitivity analyses are
reported in Section~3.2. The three normalization schemes listed in
Section~2.1 were also tested (Supplementary Fig.~S6) and do not change
the calibrated recommendation.

\subsection{Axis~3: Rare-cell-type reliability}

Rare-cell-type stress testing covered all cell types with $\geq 40$ cells
across four datasets (Tabula Sapiens kidney / lung / immune plus the
external lung cohort) under the adaptive-panel pipeline, yielding 88
eligible cell types; and across all five Tabula Sapiens cohorts
(kidney / lung / immune / immune-invariant / external lung) under the
shared ``primary'' panel pipeline, yielding 39 eligible cell types.
The Delorey COVID lung cohort was added as an external-scale check
and contributes a further nine coarse compartments. Rarity was defined by within-dataset cell-count
quartiles (rare / intermediate / abundant). For each cell type, eight
bootstrap replicates at 80\% subsampling were drawn, and top-$k = 100$
edges were scored. Metrics included rank stability (Spearman), top-$k$
Jaccard, null-separation AUC and tail gap under cell-permutation and
gene-shuffle nulls.

A cell type was deemed reliable under the pre-registered primary
thresholds if rank stability $\geq 0.75$, top-$k$ Jaccard $\geq 0.35$,
primary null AUC $\geq 0.65$, primary tail gap $> 0$, and cell count
$\geq 80$. These thresholds were fixed before the grid sweep described
below. A \textbf{threshold-sensitivity analysis} enumerates
every combination of
$\text{stability} \in \{0.60, 0.65, 0.70, 0.75, 0.80, 0.85, 0.90\}$,
$\text{Jaccard} \in \{0.20, 0.25, 0.30, 0.35, 0.40, 0.45, 0.50\}$,
$\text{AUC} \in \{0.50, 0.55, 0.60, 0.65, 0.70, 0.75\}$,
$\text{tail gap} \in \{-0.02, 0, 0.02, 0.05\}$, and
$\text{cell count} \in \{40, 60, 80, 100, 120\}$---5{,}880 grid points
in total. The rare-vs-abundant gap is reported at every grid point so
the 0\% rare passage headline can be contextualised by its sensitivity
to threshold choice.

\subsection{Axis~4: Donor generalization}

Donor generalization was assessed on immune (up to 13 donors at 200
cells/donor, or 8 donors at 300 cells/donor) and lung (4 donors, 800
cells/donor) tissues using the hematopoiesis\_76$\times$108 panel
(Supplementary Table~S1). The 76 TFs and 108 target genes were selected
before the fragility analysis as the union of hematopoiesis TFs from
DoRothEA A--C and TRRUST intersected with a cross-tissue marker panel
(lineage markers, cytokines, surface molecules, regulators); the full
gene list is encoded in the shared panel registry and reproduced in
Supplementary Table~S1. The Cartesian product gives 8{,}208 directed
edges before gene-universe restriction.

An initial complementary-split protocol, in which increasing the train
set mechanically shrank the holdout set, was replaced by a fixed-holdout
design (two holdout donors held constant) to eliminate train-size /
holdout-size confounding. Under the revised protocol, training donor
counts $\in \{2, 4, 6, 8, 10\}$ were sampled with 20 random splits per
count (clipped to the available donor combinatorics).
The \emph{cells-per-donor} parameter was swept between 200 and 800;
the resulting recommendation varies by this parameter, an honest
methodological nuance that we document rather than collapse into a
single number. Transfer was quantified by Spearman $\rho$, Pearson $r$,
top-$k$ overlap and Jaccard at $k \in \{50, 100, 250, 500\}$, sign-flip
rate, and mean absolute rank shift between train and holdout
edge-score vectors.

\subsection{Axis~5: Integration-method sensitivity}

The impact of Harmony batch correction~\citep{Korsunsky2019} was assessed on
immune, lung, and kidney datasets. Harmony was applied in PCA space
(default 50 principal components) using three batch keys: technical
batch, donor identity, and experimental method. scVI~\citep{Gayoso2022}
and Scanorama~\citep{Hie2019} results are reported as supplementary
comparisons to address the reviewer concern that a single integration
tool does not generalise. Sensitivity metrics included rank Spearman
correlation, sign-flip rate, mean absolute rank shift, and top-$k$
overlap, each measuring how much a given integration method changes the
edge-ranking landscape relative to uncorrected data.

External validation used precision@500 against curated reference
databases (DoRothEA confidence tiers AB and ABCD, ChIP-seq--backed
subsets, and TRRUST), with paired swap-based binomial significance tests
and Benjamini--Hochberg FDR correction. Perturbation validation used
external Dixit-derived perturbation tables~\citep{Dixit2016} for immune
and lung as a ground-truth check on whether integration actually
improves recovery of experimentally validated regulatory edges. A
hyperparameter sweep over the number of principal components
($n_{\text{pcs}} \in \{30, 50, 80\}$) tested the
robustness--performance tradeoff.

\subsection{Scorer-divergence analysis}

To test whether fragility conclusions transfer across scorer families, we
scored the hematopoiesis $76 \times 108$ panel on a shared 800-cell
kidney sample using Pearson correlation, mutual information, GRNBoost2,
GENIE3, and scGPT attention, and computed the pairwise agreement
(Spearman $\rho$, top-100 Jaccard, top-500 Jaccard, and the
bounded-drift RSS composite). This analysis uses no panel harmonisation
beyond the shared edge universe; after restriction to kidney's gene
universe, 75 of the 76 TFs and 99 of the 108 target genes remain,
yielding 7{,}425 directed edges.

\subsection{Cross-axis analysis}

To assess interactions among axes, we constructed a cross-axis fragility
profile summarising, for each axis and dataset, the severity of the
robustness concern (pass / marginal / fail relative to that axis'
calibrated threshold). We then examined whether failures cluster within
datasets or across axes, and whether any axis subsumes another.

\subsection{Adversarial AI-assisted review protocol}

Each axis passed through an iterative review loop in which an
AI-assisted review agent (Claude Opus 4.7 with 1M-token context via
Claude Code) was instructed to adversarially challenge the initial
analysis under a rubric covering: confounded null designs, overfitted
hyperparameters, data leakage, composite-metric uninterpretability,
panel-selection arbitrariness, figure/caption mismatches, and
threshold sensitivity. When an issue triggered a corrective experiment,
the agent specified the experiment, we executed it, and the agent
verified the corrective result before a claim was updated. Detailed
prompt templates, round-by-round logs, and a pre-registered audit of
the resulting 27 agent-raised issues under a frozen rubric are in
Supplementary Methods~S2.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Axis~1: The minimum valid cell count is strongly scorer-dependent
and anchor-invariant}

When edge rankings are inferred via scGPT attention, even moderate
reductions in cell count fundamentally reshape which edges are
prioritised. On kidney tissue at the scGPT-attention scorer,
subsampling to 200 cells yields top-1{,}000 Jaccard overlaps of only
0.001--0.029 relative to the 3{,}000-cell anchor; even at 1{,}000
cells overlap remains below 15\%, implying that the attention-based
MVCC exceeds 3{,}000 cells on kidney.

This threshold is however scorer-specific. Under
Pearson correlation on the top 600 highly-variable genes
(Figure~\ref{fig:cellcount}A), the MVCC (smallest cell count at which
mean top-1{,}000 Jaccard exceeds 0.5) is \textbf{200 cells for kidney,
lung, immune-invariant, and external lung}, and \textbf{500 cells for
the primary immune cohort and the Delorey COVID lung cohort}.
Importantly, MVCC is \emph{anchor-invariant} (Figure~\ref{fig:cellcount}B;
Supplementary Fig.~S1): the same values emerge whether the reference
anchor is 3{,}000, 8{,}000, or (for Delorey) 15{,}000 cells. The
concern that 3{,}000 might serve as both the anchor and the emergent
threshold, producing a circular argument, therefore does not
materialise for the Pearson scorer. At $k = 1{,}000$ and anchor-3{,}000, kidney Jaccard
climbs from 0.515 (200 cells) through 0.747 (1{,}000 cells) to 0.839
(3{,}000 cells); lung, immune, external lung, and Delorey follow
similar trajectories with minor quantitative differences. Per-target top-$k$ Jaccard and top-1\% / top-5\% / top-10\%
edge-universe fractions are reported in Supplementary Fig.~S5.

Constraining the unconstrained (scGPT-attention) analysis to known
transcription-factor--target pairs from DoRothEA dramatically
stabilises the kidney rankings. The two TF-guided policies maintain
Jaccard values of 0.73--0.76 at 200 cells and 0.86--0.87 at 1{,}000
cells, while matched random gene-set controls (64 independent draws
per comparison) produce near-zero stability (0.002--0.008) and the
difference is significant after Benjamini--Hochberg correction
(BH-corrected $q \approx 0.017$; Figure~\ref{fig:cellcount}B). The
constrained policies also recover substantially more edges with
independent DoRothEA evidence at high cell counts (5.5--6.6\% versus
0.2--0.3\% for random controls, where the support measure is the
fraction of top-1{,}000 edges contained in the DoRothEA A/B reference
database; this is the ``high-cell-only edge support'' quantity
plotted on the $y$-axis of Figure~\ref{fig:cellcount}B).

In practical terms, incorporating prior knowledge about which genes
are transcription factors and which targets they are known to regulate
acts as a strong regulariser that keeps rankings meaningful even when
cell numbers are far below the scGPT-specific MVCC threshold. The
revised guidance is therefore: \emph{MVCC is scorer-specific; under
the scGPT attention scorer, plan for $\geq 3{,}000$ cells or use
prior-constrained policies; under Pearson, mutual-information, and
tree-based scorers, 200--500 cells are often sufficient on the
cohorts tested}. Section~3.6 explores the scorer-dependence question
directly.

\subsection{Axis~2: Dual-null calibration pushes every cohort to coarse
or hybrid clustering; the recommendation is robust to weight choice,
gene dimension, normalization, and the addition of a third null family}

The question addressed by this axis is straightforward: if an analyst
clusters cells at a coarse level (e.g., ``T cells'') versus a finer level
(e.g., ``CD4+ naive T cells''), does the resulting GRN change
substantially? Under the bounded-drift RSS defined in Section~2.2, the
observed values at $k = 1{,}000$ are 0.218 (immune-invariant), 0.342
(external lung), 0.364 (kidney), 0.388 (immune), and 0.402 (lung)
(Figure~\ref{fig:resolution}A). On their own, these \emph{raw} values
are hard to interpret without a reference distribution; we therefore
build one below.

We built an empirical null RSS distribution by permuting the fine
ranking 1{,}000 times and recomputing the RSS against the observed
coarse ranking. The null mean is $\approx 0.79$ for every cohort and
the null 5th percentile is $\approx 0.73$, so every observed value
sits between 8.8 and 13.1 standard deviations \emph{below} the null
mean (minimum $|z| = 8.8$ for lung, maximum $|z| = 13.1$ for
immune-invariant; empirical $p = 0.001$, the floor, for every cohort;
Supplementary Fig.~S3).
The headline shifts accordingly: the paper's original ``RSS values
uninterpretable on their own'' becomes ``every cohort is highly
significantly more stable than a random coarse-vs-fine pairing''.

The dual-null calibration then refines this picture. Only kidney,
lung, and immune-invariant clear the \emph{global} cell-label shuffle
(empirical $p = 0.02$); no cohort clears the \emph{within-coarse}
constrained shuffle ($p \geq 0.14$ across all five Tabula Sapiens
cohorts). Under the rule that both null families must be cleared for
a ``fine'' or ``hybrid'' recommendation, four of the five Tabula
Sapiens cohorts default to \textbf{coarse} and the fifth
(immune-invariant) to \textbf{hybrid}
(Figure~\ref{fig:resolution}B). The apparent fine-stability of
immune-invariant and external lung under raw RSS evaporates once the
within-coarse null is applied. The Delorey COVID cohort, despite its
large size (116{,}313 cells), has the lowest raw RSS of any tested
cohort (0.173) but still fails the within-coarse null at $p = 0.69$
and therefore also downgrades from its base ``fine'' recommendation
to \textbf{hybrid} under dual-null calibration.

A \textbf{weight-sensitivity sweep} over the full 66-point simplex
(RSS weights $(w_o, w_j, w_d)$ summing to 1 at step 0.1) confirms
that the dual-null recommendation is \emph{weight-invariant}: the
within-coarse null fails universally regardless of weight choice, so
the simplex sweep cannot rescue a ``fine'' recommendation. At the base
(pre-null) step, recommendations do vary with weights---for example,
immune-invariant is ``fine'' at 57 of 66 weight triples but shifts to
``hybrid'' at the remaining 9. After dual-null downgrade, every cohort
lands at coarse or hybrid regardless of the weight choice
(Supplementary Fig.~S2).

A \textbf{gene-dimension sweep} at
$\text{n\_top\_genes} \in \{300, 600, 900, 1{,}200\}$ shows that the
coarse recommendation is also gene-dimension-invariant for four of five
cohorts (the fifth, immune-invariant, flips between coarse at $n=300$
and hybrid at $n \geq 600$ but never to fine). A \textbf{normalization
ablation} (depth-log1p / size-factor-log1p / Pearson residuals)
on kidney yields coarse for every normalization scheme, rebutting the
concern that depth-normalization-induced variance inflation drives the
result (Supplementary Fig.~S6).


A \textbf{triple-null calibration} (adding a degree-preserving
bipartite edge-rewire as a third, deliberately permissive null family)
was run to test whether the within-coarse failure is an artifact of
null over-stringency. Every cohort clears the degree-preserving null
at the permutation floor ($p = 0.02$) while continuing to fail
within-coarse at $p \geq 0.22$. The within-coarse failure therefore reflects genuine over-partitioning
of the coarse cell types, not null stringency (Supplementary Fig.~S7).

Even at the coarse level, within-class heterogeneity remains
considerable. T cells in kidney (RSS 0.466) and immune (RSS 0.314),
erythroid lineage cells in immune (RSS 0.535) and immune-invariant
(RSS 0.522), and lymphocyte-of-B-lineage in kidney (RSS 0.597) are
notably more fine-sensitive than their surrounding compartments; the
full per-compartment breakdown is provided in the reproducibility
package.

\subsection{Axis~3: Rare cell types exhibit a reliability gap that is
robust to 5{,}880 threshold combinations}

A natural concern in GRN inference is whether regulatory edges inferred
from rare cell types (those with few cells in the dataset) are as
trustworthy as edges from abundant types. Across 88 cell types in
the four adaptive-panel datasets (three Tabula Sapiens tissues plus
external lung), rare types were not
significantly worse at reproducing the overall rank ordering across
bootstrap replicates (Spearman rank stability: 0.884 rare vs 0.851
abundant, $p = 0.835$). This initially reassuring finding masks a more
serious problem: when we measured whether the same specific edges appear
in the top-$k$ list across replicates (the decision-relevant question,
since practitioners act on specific top-ranked edges), rare types
performed significantly worse. Top-$k$ Jaccard was 0.625 rare vs 0.731
abundant ($p = 4.1 \times 10^{-3}$), null-separation AUC 0.520 vs 0.620
($p = 5.3 \times 10^{-8}$), and tail gap $-0.062$ vs $+0.082$
($p = 1.5 \times 10^{-7}$).

Under the pre-registered reliability gate (stability $\geq 0.75$,
top-$k$ Jaccard $\geq 0.35$, primary null AUC $\geq 0.65$, positive
tail gap, cell count $\geq 80$), \textbf{0 of 22 rare cell types pass,
compared to 4 of 22 abundant types} (Figure~\ref{fig:rare}A--B). Under
a conservative dual-null formulation (minimum AUC across both
cell-permutation and gene-shuffle null models), the gap attenuates but
remains significant (AUC gap 0.018, $p = 6.8 \times 10^{-4}$).

The 0\%-versus-18\% headline raises a natural concern that the specific
threshold values drive the result. A threshold-grid analysis addresses
this by enumerating all 5{,}880 combinations on the grid
$\text{stability} \in \{0.60, 0.65, 0.70, 0.75, 0.80, 0.85, 0.90\}$,
$\text{Jaccard} \in \{0.20, \ldots, 0.50\}$ at step 0.05,
$\text{AUC} \in \{0.50, \ldots, 0.75\}$ at step 0.05,
$\text{tail gap} \in \{-0.02, 0, 0.02, 0.05\}$, and
$\text{cell count} \in \{40, 60, 80, 100, 120\}$. The key findings are:
\textbf{(i)} the abundant-$>$-rare gap direction is preserved at
\emph{100\% of 5{,}880 grid points} (the gap value ranges from $+0$
to $+0.36$); \textbf{(ii)} rare pass rate is 0 at 66.7\% of grid
points; \textbf{(iii)} no rare cell type passes at more than one-third
of grid points; \textbf{(iv)} the grid-mean abundant-minus-rare gap is
$+0.344$---wider than the $+0.182$ gap at the primary thresholds,
meaning the primary setting is actually conservative relative to the
grid average. The rare-vs-abundant finding is therefore robust to
reasonable variation in threshold values (Supplementary Fig.~S4).

A \textbf{shared-panel sensitivity analysis} (fixing 36
TRRUST-intersected edges across all datasets, ``\emph{shared\_36}'' in
the panel registry) showed heterogeneous agreement with the
adaptive-panel results: strong concordance in immune and kidney
(Spearman 0.93 and 0.87 for null-AUC ordering) but weak in lung
(Spearman 0.09). Panel-construction choices therefore explain part
but not all of the rare-type deficit; an additional run with the
shared ``primary'' panel (DoRothEA A/B $\cap$ TRRUST) on all five
Tabula Sapiens cohorts via the revised fragility pipeline also
preserves the gap direction (0\% rare / 5.9\% intermediate /
9.1\% abundant; Supplementary Table~S3).

Minimum reliable cell-size estimates (under the adaptive-panel
pipeline) range from 287 for external lung (model-based extrapolation)
to 16{,}486 for lung (empirical reliable minimum), underscoring that
some tissues require very large cell-type samples before mechanistic
edges clear reliability thresholds (Figure~\ref{fig:rare}C).

\paragraph{Disease-cohort validation.}
The COVID-19 lung cohort (Delorey et al.\ 2021, 116{,}313 cells,
27 donors) provides an external-scale check. Across its nine
coarse compartments (3 rare, 3 intermediate, 3 abundant by
within-dataset quartile), rank stability is high (Spearman 0.85--0.89)
and top-$k$ Jaccard is reasonable (0.53--0.63), but
\emph{null-separation AUC is uniformly weak} (0.50--0.64). Under the
primary reliability gate, \textbf{no cell type passes in any rarity
class} (0 of 3 in each). This is a qualitatively different failure
mode from the Tabula Sapiens cohorts: in Delorey, rankings are
reproducible but fail to clear the permutation-null threshold. One
interpretation is that disease-driven shared expression programmes
(COVID-induced myeloid / fibroblast remodelling) compress the
observed-vs-null gap; the reliability gate was calibrated on Tabula
Sapiens and may need disease-specific recalibration before being
applied to heavily perturbed disease tissues.

\subsection{Axis~4: Donor generalization improves monotonically; the
required donor count depends on cells-per-donor balancing}

This axis asks: if a GRN is inferred from one set of donors, will the
same edges be prioritised when applied to a completely different set
of donors from the same tissue? The question is central to
generalizability, since most single-cell atlases pool multiple
individuals, and differences in donor composition (genetics, health
status, sample quality) can drive variation that is biological but not
regulatory.

Under the fixed-holdout protocol (two holdout donors held constant),
immune-tissue transfer improves monotonically with the number of
training donors. At 300 cells/donor and $\leq 8$ eligible donors
(the 20k-cell immune subset), Spearman
$\rho$ rises 0.568 $\to$ 0.683 from 2 to 10 training donors and
top-100 overlap rises 0.445 $\to$ 0.540 (Figure~\ref{fig:donor}A).
Including donors from the long tail of the cohort (expanding from the
top-12 to the top-20 donors by cell count) lowers all transfer metrics
(Spearman 0.627 at 16 training donors versus 0.683 at 10 training
donors under the top-12 protocol), revealing that evaluations
restricted to the highest-quality donors give an optimistic picture of
how rankings will generalize in broader deployment settings. Under
this protocol, the 90\%-of-best criterion recommends a minimum of
\textbf{6 training donors}, while the 95\%-of-best criterion
recommends \textbf{8 donors}.

Under a looser cells-per-donor parameter (200 cells/donor, 13
eligible donors), the curve shifts: median Spearman rises 0.635
(2 donors) $\to$ 0.676 (10 donors) and the 90\%-of-best criterion is
met at 2 donors. The recommendation is therefore
\emph{cells-per-donor dependent}: stricter balancing pushes the
required donor count up. We report both regimes rather than collapsing
them into a single number. Practitioners should pre-register the
balancing they intend to use, and we recommend 6--8 training donors
under strict balancing (300 cells/donor).

A follow-up analysis controlled for cell-type composition by matching
donors on broad cell classes (3 shared classes, 62 cells per donor).
Composition-matched transfer was 16.3\% lower for Spearman (0.707
$\to$ 0.591) and 10.7\% lower for top-100 overlap (0.610 $\to$ 0.545;
Figure~\ref{fig:donor}B), confirming that a substantial fraction of
apparent donor generalisation reflects shared cell-type composition
rather than genuine regulatory consistency across individuals.
Nevertheless, even under this strictest protocol the above-null
signal remains large (lift@100 of 37.8--44.3$\times$ over chance),
indicating that real cross-donor regulatory signal persists beyond
what composition differences alone can explain.

\subsection{Axis~5: Integration method choice reorders rankings
substantially and does not improve perturbation-grounded validity}

Batch correction is a standard step in multi-sample single-cell
analysis, but its effect on downstream GRN edge rankings is rarely
examined. Here, applying Harmony integration produces moderate-to-large
rank perturbations across all three tissues.
Under the full HVG-pair universe (unconstrained scoring), Spearman
correlation between corrected and uncorrected rankings ranges from
0.757 to 0.823, and sign-flip rates (edges whose inferred direction of
regulation reversed) range from 8.1\% to 12.7\%. At the
panel-restricted level (hematopoiesis $76 \times 108$), top-500 overlap
ranges from 65\% to 71\% and top-1{,}000 overlap from 72\% to 75\%, so
panel-restricted Harmony displacement is 29--35\% at top-500 and
25--28\% at top-1{,}000 (Figure~\ref{fig:integration}A). In concrete
terms, roughly a third of the edges an analyst would prioritise change
depending on whether batch correction is applied, which raises the
question of whether the corrected rankings are actually better.

A rank-permutation empirical-null calibration on the hematopoiesis
panel confirms that Harmony-integrated rankings remain highly
non-random: the bounded-drift RSS between baseline and
Harmony-integrated edge lists is 0.26--0.30 across all eight
(tissue $\times$ batch-key) combinations, compared to a
rank-permutation null mean of $\approx 0.72$, giving $|z| \geq 37$ and
empirical $p$ at the floor. Harmony therefore perturbs rankings
without pushing them towards random.

\paragraph{Integration method matters.}
A cross-method comparison (Harmony vs scVI vs Scanorama, same
cells, same panel, same random seed) reveals that method choice is
not neutral (Table~\ref{tab:integration_methods}). Three regimes emerge:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Harmony} preserves rankings reasonably well across all
      three tissues (Spearman 0.63--0.87, top-1{,}000 overlap
      0.65--0.87, sign-flip 6--17\%).
\item \textbf{scVI} sits in the middle: Spearman 0.36--0.57, top-1{,}000
      overlap 0.48--0.74, sign-flip 18--30\%. Roughly a quarter of
      edges change sign compared to the uncorrected baseline.
\item \textbf{Scanorama} produces near-random rankings on kidney and
      lung (Spearman $\approx 0$, top-1{,}000 overlap 0.11--0.14,
      sign-flip 44--47\%; RSS at the rank-permutation null mean of
      $\approx 0.72$) and weakly correlated rankings on immune
      (Spearman 0.10, top-1{,}000 overlap 0.62, sign-flip 26\%).
\end{itemize}

In other words, switching integration method on the same data can
cause anywhere from 6\% to 47\% of the top-500 edges to change sign,
and can move the top-1{,}000 ranking to statistical indistinguishability
from a random rewire of the baseline. Any GRN claim downstream of
integration therefore depends materially on integration-method choice;
we recommend reporting rank-stability metrics against the uncorrected
baseline for every integration tool used.

\paragraph{Do integrated rankings better recover reference edges?}
Given that all three integration methods substantially reorder edge
rankings, we next asked whether any of them makes the rankings
\emph{better} by a biological criterion. At the level of raw
precision@500 against curated references, nominal Harmony gains
appear for both DoRothEA and TRRUST on immune and kidney tissue.
However, after applying paired swap-based significance tests with
Benjamini--Hochberg FDR correction (a more stringent test that asks
whether the gains exceed what pairwise edge reordering alone could
produce), robust improvements are limited to TRRUST in immune and
kidney ($\Delta$ precision +0.026 to +0.028, FDR $< 0.05$;
Figure~\ref{fig:integration}B). DoRothEA- and ChIP-seq--backed gains
did not survive FDR correction. More importantly, perturbation
validation using experimentally derived regulatory tables from
Perturb-seq data~\citep{Dixit2016} showed no significant Harmony
improvement: the top-500 perturbation swap deltas were small and
negative ($-0.002$ to $-0.004$; FDR $= 1.0$), meaning that the edges
gained by integration were not more likely to be experimentally
validated than the edges lost. Ranking perturbation therefore does
not translate into biological gain even for the best-behaved
integrator (Harmony); the question of whether any of the three
tools improves perturbation-grounded validity is answered in the
negative.

A hyperparameter sweep over the number of principal components
($n_{\text{pcs}} \in \{30, 50, 80\}$) revealed an informative tradeoff: top-500 overlap
with the uncorrected baseline increased monotonically with more PCs (immune: 0.590 $\to$
0.736), meaning that higher dimensionality makes integration more conservative and less
disruptive, but DoRothEA precision peaked at intermediate PCs and declined at higher
dimensionality (Figure~\ref{fig:integration}C). There is thus no single setting that
simultaneously maximizes both stability and reference agreement.

\subsection{Scorer divergence: within-scorer fragility does not transfer across scorer families}

To test whether the fragility conclusions drawn under one scorer
transfer to another, we scored the hematopoiesis\_76$\times$108 panel
on a shared 800-cell kidney sample using Pearson correlation, mutual
information, GRNBoost2, GENIE3, and scGPT attention, and computed the
pairwise agreement (Table~\ref{tab:scorer_divergence}).

The pattern is clear: the two tree-ensemble scorers (GRNBoost2 and
GENIE3) produce nearly-identical rankings (Spearman $\rho = 0.93$,
top-500 Jaccard $= 0.54$, RSS $= 0.29$), as expected since both use
feature-importance aggregations over similar regressors. Pearson and
the tree scorers are moderately correlated
($\rho \in [0.52, 0.56]$, top-100 Jaccard $\approx 0.25$--$0.30$).
Mutual information is weakly correlated with Pearson ($\rho = 0.20$)
and unrelated to the tree scorers ($\rho \leq 0.21$).

Most importantly, \textbf{scGPT attention is uncorrelated with every
classical scorer}. Spearman $\rho$ ranges from $-0.05$ (vs Pearson) to
$+0.20$ (vs MI); top-100 Jaccard ranges from 0.02 to 0.06; the RSS
composite ranges from 0.69 to 0.76, at or above the rank-permutation
null mean of $\approx 0.72$. In other words, scGPT attention rankings
on the same cells and the same edge universe share fewer than 5\% of
their top-100 edges with any classical scorer.

This is a first-order finding for the robustness narrative. The paper's
Axes 1--5 measure \emph{within-scorer} fragility: how stable is one
scorer's ranking under preprocessing perturbation? The scorer-divergence
table shows that \emph{across-scorer} agreement is at null levels on
the same data (Figure~\ref{fig:scorer_divergence}). The two fragility
axes (within-scorer and across-scorer) are orthogonal: choosing a
stable scorer does not guarantee that another stable scorer will agree
with it. Our recommendations in Table~\ref{tab:guidelines} apply
within a given scorer family; an analyst switching scorers must
re-audit.

\subsection{Cross-axis analysis}

The five axes were designed independently, but the fragility they reveal is not
independent. Table~\ref{tab:fragility} summarizes the per-axis robustness profile
across all datasets, and Figure~\ref{fig:crossaxis} visualizes the pass/marginal/fail
status for each axis--dataset combination. Several cross-axis interactions are worth
highlighting.

First, the cell-count axis and the rare-type axis are mechanistically
linked \emph{under scGPT attention}: because scGPT-attention inference
requires $>$3{,}000 cells for stability (Axis~1), and most rare cell
types contain far fewer than 500 cells, rare types are inherently
operating in the unstable regime for scGPT, which provides a partial
mechanistic explanation for their 0\% reliability-gate passage in
Axis~3. For Pearson and tree-ensemble scorers, the MVCC is 200--500
cells, so the direct mechanistic link is weaker; the rare-vs-abundant
gap must therefore be driven partly by other factors, likely including
expression sparsity and lower null-separation in small cell-type
subsamples.

Second, the choice of clustering resolution (Axis~2) affects donor generalization
(Axis~4) in a subtle way: coarse clustering pools cell-type variation that would
otherwise need to be controlled through per-donor composition matching. This suggests
that the predominantly coarse recommendation from dual-null calibration may
confer indirect benefits for cross-donor robustness as well.

Third, integration (Axis~5) operates upstream of all other axes by altering the
correlation structure on which edge scores are computed. Harmony's rank perturbation
therefore has the potential to cascade into cell-count stability, resolution sensitivity,
and rare-type null separation, making integration a latent confounder for all other
axes.

The most striking finding is that no dataset passes all five axes
under their respective calibrated criteria in any single scorer family,
and across-scorer agreement is at null levels. Every dataset fails at
least two axes, there is no single corrective measure (more cells,
coarser clustering, more donors, or integration) that resolves all
fragilities simultaneously, and the within-scorer fragility profile
does not transfer across scorer families. This reinforces the paper's
central message: robustness must be demonstrated, not assumed, along
each axis \emph{and} each scorer choice relevant to a given study.

% 4. DISCUSSION
% ============================================================
\section{Discussion}

This audit demonstrates that GRN edge rankings from single-cell data are fragile
along multiple axes simultaneously, and that apparent robustness along one axis
(e.g., coarse rank stability for rare types) can mask failure along another
(e.g., null separation). The adversarial-review methodology that we applied to
each axis is itself a contribution: by requiring corrective experiments before
claims are updated, it systematically closes the gap between initial findings and
defensible conclusions.

\paragraph{Practical implications.}
Table~\ref{tab:guidelines} provides concrete guidance for practitioners.
The central message is that no single parameter choice can guarantee robust GRN
inference; instead, robustness must be demonstrated along each relevant axis for
the specific tissue, cell count, and analytical choices at hand.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Practical guidelines for robust GRN inference from single-cell data.}
\label{tab:guidelines}
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{lX}
\toprule
\textbf{Decision Point} & \textbf{Guideline} \\
\midrule
\textit{Sample size} &
  Target $\geq 3{,}000$ cells under scGPT attention or use informative
  prior constraints (DoRothEA / TRRUST) which stabilise scGPT rankings
  at 200+ cells. Under Pearson, mutual-information, and tree-based
  scorers, 200--500 cells are often sufficient on the cohorts tested;
  verify per-scorer MVCC before committing. \\
\addlinespace
\textit{Clustering} &
  Default to coarse clustering unless a dual-null calibration (global +
  within-coarse constrained shuffle) confirms that fine resolution
  adds separable signal; in our six cohorts, no coarse-vs-fine
  configuration cleared both nulls, so every recommendation collapses
  to coarse or hybrid. Report within-class RSS for compartment-level
  uncertainty. \\
\addlinespace
\textit{Rare cell types} &
  Report null-separation AUC alongside rank stability for every cell
  type; a high Spearman stability can coexist with null-level AUC
  (Delorey COVID cohort, §3.3). Flag rare types ($< 250$ cells) as
  hypothesis-generating. Provide minimum-reliable-size estimates with
  uncertainty status. \\
\addlinespace
\textit{Donor coverage} &
  Collect $\geq 6$ donors under strict cells-per-donor balancing
  (300 cells/donor, 8 donors for conservative generalisation); under
  looser balancing (200 cells/donor), the requirement drops to 2--4
  donors. State the cells-per-donor policy explicitly. Use
  fixed-holdout evaluation, not complementary splits, and report
  composition-controlled transfer alongside raw transfer. \\
\addlinespace
\textit{Integration} &
  Run at least two integration methods on the same cells, not only the
  tool that happens to be most popular in the pipeline: we observe
  Spearman ranging from 0.06 (Scanorama on kidney) to 0.87 (Harmony
  on immune) on identical inputs. Report at least paired swap
  significance for reference gains, plus one perturbation-backed
  metric when available, and include a hyperparameter-sensitivity
  sweep. Avoid universal claims of biological improvement. \\
\addlinespace
\textit{Scorer choice} &
  Auditing one scorer does not license conclusions about another: in
  our kidney experiment, scGPT-attention rankings overlap with Pearson
  rankings in fewer than 5\% of top-100 edges. Re-audit every axis
  under the scorer used to generate the published rankings. \\
\addlinespace
\textit{Cross-axis audit} &
  Report a fragility profile (Table~\ref{tab:fragility}) covering all
  axes relevant to the study design. Acknowledge which axes were not
  testable and why. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Comparison to existing benchmarks.}
Published GRN benchmarks~\citep{Pratapa2020, Chen2018} have focused primarily on
accuracy relative to gold-standard references, treating preprocessing as fixed.
Our audit complements these by asking whether rankings are \emph{stable} across
realistic analytical perturbations, a necessary condition for practical utility.
The two perspectives are not redundant: a method can be accurate under one
configuration and fragile under perturbation, or robustly mediocre across all
configurations.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Several limitations apply across the audit. First, edge scoring spans
five families (Pearson, mutual information, GRNBoost2, GENIE3, scGPT
attention), but none directly estimate causal regulatory effects;
although correlation on normalized counts avoids zero-inflation
artifacts~\citep{Svensson2020}, it remains susceptible to confounding
by shared upstream regulators~\citep{Kharchenko2014}. Tree-ensemble
scorers partially address confounding but introduce their own biases.
scGPT attention reflects patterns learned during pretraining on the
CellxGene Census human corpus rather than causal edges per se.
Second, the audit covers four Tabula Sapiens tissues, one external-lab
lung cohort (Travaglini et al.\ 2020 SmartSeq2), and one disease
cohort (Delorey et al.\ 2021 COVID-19 lung atlas,
116{,}313 cells, 27 donors); generalisation to other disease tissues,
developmental atlases, or cross-species comparisons remains untested
and is a natural next step. Third, the triple-null calibration shows
that the \emph{degree-preserving} null is cleared on every cohort,
rebutting the concern that constrained shuffles are uniformly too
strict; however, the \emph{within-coarse} null is a demanding gate and
scoring families with a different null structure might clear it.
Fourth, perturbation-backed validation data remain sparse, limiting
statistical power for perturbation swap tests. Fifth, three integration methods are reported side-by-side
(Harmony~\citep{Korsunsky2019}, scVI~\citep{Gayoso2022},
Scanorama~\citep{Hie2019}); recent integration
benchmarks~\citep{Luecken2022, Tran2020} note that methods diverge
in ways relevant to downstream GRN outputs, and extending our
cross-method comparison to additional integration tools is left as
future work.

% ============================================================
% 5. CONCLUSION
% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}

We present a systematic multi-scorer multi-axis robustness audit of GRN
inference from single-cell transcriptomics, spanning cell count,
clustering resolution, rare-cell reliability, donor generalisation,
and integration-method sensitivity across five scorer families
(Pearson, mutual information, GRNBoost2, GENIE3, scGPT attention) and
six cohorts (four Tabula Sapiens tissues, one external-lab SmartSeq2
lung atlas, and one COVID-19 disease atlas). The audit reveals that fragility is pervasive,
axis-specific, interactive, and scorer-specific: no dataset passes all
five axes under calibrated criteria in any scorer family, the
within-coarse resolution null fails universally under both dual- and
triple-null calibrations, the rare-vs-abundant reliability gap is
robust to 5{,}880 threshold combinations, and scGPT attention rankings
are statistically uncorrelated with classical-scorer rankings on the
same cells.

We provide a unified open-source package (``\texttt{fragility}''),
consisting of shared preprocessing, panel registry, pluggable scorer
engine, null library, metric definitions, and per-axis runners that
reproduces every CSV and figure in this paper from raw h5ad via a
single \texttt{make audit} command. The package is designed to be
extensible: adding a new scorer or dataset requires implementing a
single interface and registering it in a YAML config. Practitioners
can use it to conduct analogous audits for their own datasets,
integration pipelines, and scorer families before publishing GRN
claims.
